# Domain Pack: Accounting

Scenarios for an accounting course. The student is the accountant classifying a transaction, deciding when to recognize it, or reading what a statement says about a firm.

## Scenario entities and decisions

- A firm recording transactions and the student deciding the accounts and the timing.
- A set of statements (income statement, balance sheet, statement of cash flows) to read or reconcile.
- A judgment call on recognition: when revenue is earned, when an expense is matched, what is capitalized versus expensed.
- A ratio analysis of a company's liquidity, solvency, or profitability.

## Metrics, frameworks, and vocabulary (with realistic ranges)

- Foundations: the accounting equation (assets equal liabilities plus equity), debits and credits, journal entries and T-accounts, the three financial statements and how they connect, accrual versus cash basis, the revenue-recognition and matching principles, depreciation.
- Ratios: current ratio (often 1.5 to 3), quick ratio, debt-to-equity, gross margin, operating margin, return on assets, return on equity, inventory turnover.

## What good distractors look like here

- Confusing profit with cash: a profitable firm can run out of cash, and recording revenue is not the same as receiving cash.
- Recognizing revenue when cash arrives rather than when it is earned (or the reverse) under accrual rules.
- Expensing something that should be capitalized, or vice versa.
- Misclassifying which statement an item belongs on (an accrued liability on the income statement).
- A debit-equals-decrease misconception, or reversing the normal balance of an account.

## House style: two worked examples

Scenario, medium: "A firm signs a $12,000 annual contract on December 1 and collects the full amount upfront. Under accrual accounting, how much revenue is recognized in December?" Correct answer: $1,000, because revenue is recognized as it is earned over the service period, not when cash is collected; the rest is deferred (unearned) revenue, a liability. Distractors: $12,000 (cash-basis error), $0 (ignores the earned month), $11,000 (inverts the split).

Scenario, medium: "A company reports rising net income but falling cash. Which statement explains the gap, and what is a likely cause?" Correct answer: the statement of cash flows; accrual income can rise while cash falls if receivables or inventory grew or a large non-cash item was recorded. Distractors: the balance sheet alone, the income statement alone, or the conclusion that the income figure must be wrong.

## Feedback voice

Correct-answer feedback names the principle (recognition timing, the equation, which statement). Incorrect-answer feedback names the confusion, for example profit versus cash or accrual versus cash basis, without restating the answer.
