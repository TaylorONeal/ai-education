# Domain Pack: UX Design

Scenarios for a user-experience course. The student is the designer choosing a research method, judging a design against principles, or reading usability evidence.

## Scenario entities and decisions

- A product team choosing how to learn about users (interview, usability test, survey, analytics).
- An interface critiqued against usability heuristics and accessibility standards.
- A flow or information architecture with a friction point to diagnose.
- A decision between what users say they want and what the data shows they do.

## Metrics, frameworks, and vocabulary (with realistic ranges)

- Principles: Nielsen's usability heuristics, Norman's affordances and signifiers, recognition over recall, error prevention, consistency, feedback, cognitive load, Fitts's law, Hick's law.
- Methods: user interviews, usability testing, contextual inquiry, surveys, A/B testing, personas, journey maps, card sorting, wireframe-prototype-fidelity progression.
- Metrics: task success rate, time on task, error rate, the System Usability Scale (0 to 100, around 68 average), conversion, accessibility contrast ratios (4.5:1 for normal text).

## What good distractors look like here

- Confusing UX (the whole experience) with UI (the visual layer).
- Trusting what users say they prefer over what they do in a task (preference versus performance).
- Picking a method that cannot answer the question (a survey to learn why a task fails, when a usability test is needed).
- Designing for the designer's own intuition rather than evidence about users.
- Treating aesthetics as proof of usability, or assuming more features improve the experience.

## House style: two worked examples

Scenario, medium: "The team wants to know why users abandon a checkout. Which method gives the strongest evidence, and why?" Correct answer: a moderated usability test on the checkout flow, because watching real users attempt the task reveals where and why they fail, which a preference survey cannot. Distractors: a satisfaction survey (self-report, not behavior), a focus group on color, asking the team to guess.

Scenario, medium: "A button does nothing visible when tapped, so users tap it repeatedly. Which heuristic is violated?" Correct answer: visibility of system status (feedback); the interface must show that the action was received. Distractors: aesthetic minimalism, user control and freedom, error prevention.

## Feedback voice

Correct-answer feedback names the heuristic or method and why it fits. Incorrect-answer feedback names the confusion, for example preference versus performance or UX versus UI, without restating the answer.
