# Domain Pack: Business Communications

Scenarios for a business-communications course. The student is the writer or speaker choosing structure, channel, and tone to fit an audience and a purpose.

## Scenario entities and decisions

- A writer deciding how to open a message: lead with the point, or ease into bad news.
- A channel choice for a message of a given sensitivity and urgency.
- A revision that has to become clearer, more concise, and more audience-centered.
- A persuasive message that must move a specific reader to act.

## Metrics, frameworks, and vocabulary (with realistic ranges)

- Structure: the direct approach (bottom line up front) versus the indirect approach for bad news or resistant audiences, the executive summary, AIDA for persuasion (attention, interest, desire, action).
- Principles: audience analysis, purpose, the "you" attitude (audience-centered framing), clarity and conciseness, active voice, specific evidence over vague claims, the so-what test, appropriate tone and register, features versus benefits.
- Channel: when to use email, a memo, a call, or an in-person conversation based on sensitivity and the need for a record.

## What good distractors look like here

- Burying the lead: making a busy reader hunt for the point that belongs up front.
- Writer-centered framing ("I need") instead of audience-centered ("you will").
- Using the indirect approach for routine good news, or the direct approach for delicate bad news, getting the match backward.
- Choosing the wrong channel (email for a sensitive personnel matter, a call where a written record is needed).
- Listing features when the reader needs the benefit, or hedging with vague, passive language.

## House style: two worked examples

Scenario, medium: "You must tell a client a project will slip two weeks. Which opening fits, and why?" Correct answer: a brief buffer then the news with a plan, using the indirect approach, because leading bluntly with bad news to an external party can read as careless; the structure should soften and then give the date and the recovery plan. Distractors: bottom-line-up-front blunt opening (mismatched for sensitive external bad news), hide the delay, open with unrelated praise.

Scenario, medium: "A status email opens with three paragraphs of background before the one decision the reader must make. What principle is violated, and what is the fix?" Correct answer: it buries the lead; put the decision and the deadline up front (bottom line up front), then the background for those who want it. Distractors: the tone is wrong, it is too short, it needs more background.

## Feedback voice

Correct-answer feedback names the principle (direct versus indirect, the "you" attitude, bottom line up front, channel fit). Incorrect-answer feedback names the error the option commits, without restating the answer.
