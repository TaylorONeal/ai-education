# Domain Pack: Product Management

Scenarios for a product-management course. The student is the PM prioritizing, defining success, or deciding what to build and why.

## Scenario entities and decisions

- A PM prioritizing a backlog against limited engineering capacity.
- A team defining success for a feature and choosing the metric that proves it.
- A discovery decision: is there a real user problem, and what is the smallest test of it.
- A roadmap or stakeholder conflict where outcomes have to win over outputs.

## Metrics, frameworks, and vocabulary (with realistic ranges)

- Frameworks: product-market fit, the north-star metric, prioritization (RICE, value versus effort, the Kano model), the minimum viable product, jobs-to-be-done, build-measure-learn, discovery versus delivery, OKRs, the pirate-metrics funnel (acquisition, activation, retention, revenue, referral).
- Metrics: activation rate, retention (day-1, day-30), engagement, conversion, churn, north-star value; the distinction between an output (a shipped feature) and an outcome (a changed user behavior).

## What good distractors look like here

- Confusing output (we shipped it) with outcome (users changed behavior, value was created).
- Prioritizing the loudest stakeholder's request over the highest expected impact.
- Treating an MVP as a small low-quality product rather than the smallest test of a hypothesis.
- Confusing a roadmap (intended outcomes over time) with a backlog (a list of work).
- Choosing a vanity metric as the success measure, or chasing feature count.

## House style: two worked examples

Scenario, medium: "A team celebrates shipping five features this quarter, but activation did not move. What is the strongest critique in PM terms?" Correct answer: the team measured output (features shipped) instead of outcome (a change in activation or another user behavior); shipping is not success unless behavior or value changed. Distractors: they shipped too few features, they should ship faster, the engineers underperformed.

Scenario, medium: "Three features compete for one sprint. Using RICE, which factor would most justify deprioritizing a high-reach idea?" Correct answer: low confidence or high effort, since RICE divides reach times impact times confidence by effort; a high-reach idea with weak evidence or huge cost can score below a smaller, surer one. Distractors: pick by reach alone, by which stakeholder asked, by what is easiest to demo.

## Feedback voice

Correct-answer feedback names the principle (outcome versus output, the prioritization factor, the funnel stage). Incorrect-answer feedback names the trap the option falls into, without giving away the answer.
