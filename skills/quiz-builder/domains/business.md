# Domain Pack: Business (Digital Analytics and Marketing)

The first domain pack for the quiz-builder skill. It tells the generator what a realistic business-analytics scenario looks like so the questions feel native to the field rather than generic. Copy this file's structure to build a pack for another domain.

## Scenario entities and decisions

Scenarios revolve around a brand or company making a decision from data:

- A direct-to-consumer brand reviewing a marketing campaign, an influencer partnership, an email send, or a paid-ads flight.
- An e-commerce team reading a conversion funnel or a checkout drop-off.
- A product team looking at activation, retention, or engagement metrics.
- A growth team prioritizing A/B tests or interpreting a finished test.
- A manager or executive drawing a conclusion from a chart that may be flawed.

The student is cast as the analyst who has to pick the right metric, read the data correctly, catch a flawed conclusion, or recommend an action grounded in the numbers.

## Metrics and vocabulary, with realistic ranges

Use these with believable values:

- Acquisition and ads: CPA ($5 to $80), ROAS (0.8x to 5x), CTR (0.5% to 5%), CPC, CPM, impressions, spend.
- Email: open rate (15% to 45%), click-through rate (1% to 5%), click-to-open rate, conversion rate, unsubscribe rate, revenue per email.
- E-commerce and funnel: conversion rate (1% to 4%), add-to-cart rate, cart abandonment, average order value, funnel pass-through by stage.
- Product and retention: DAU, MAU, DAU/MAU stickiness, day-1 and day-30 retention, churn, lifetime value, the one metric that matters.
- Influencer: cost per like or per engagement, reach versus purchase intent, engagement rate.
- Frameworks: the pirate-metrics funnel (acquisition, activation, retention, revenue, referral), correlation versus causation, statistical significance and validity.

## What good distractors look like here

The wrong options should be the mistakes students actually make:

- The right idea on the wrong metric (judging an email by open rate when click-to-open rate is the question).
- A near-miss calculation (dividing by the wrong denominator, mixing absolute counts with rates).
- Conflating correlation with causation (two things moved together, so one caused the other).
- Reading a spike or dip without using the time axis (describing the aggregate when the question is about a specific campaign day).
- Vague directional language presented as analysis ("performance improved") versus a quantified, benchmarked claim.

## House style: two worked examples

Scenario, hard: "A brand ran two influencer partnerships in one month. Influencer A: 40,000 reach, 2,000 likes, $2,000 fee. Influencer B: 12,000 reach, 1,800 likes, $900 fee. Which influencer delivered better value, and on what basis?" Correct answer names cost per engagement for both ($1.00/like vs $0.50/like) and concludes B. Distractors: pick A for higher reach (reach is not value), pick A for more total likes (ignores cost), call it a tie (ignores the math).

Transform, medium: "A student writes 'mobile traffic is 60% of total.' Which revision passes the so-what test?" Correct answer adds the comparison and the business impact (mobile converts at 1.2% versus desktop at 3.1%, a roughly $120K opportunity). Distractors: just restating the 60%, adding a number with no comparison, adding an unrelated metric.

## Feedback voice

Correct-answer feedback says why it is right and names the principle. Incorrect-answer feedback names the misconception the option represents without restating the correct answer verbatim, so a student who reads it learns the distinction rather than the answer.
