# Domain Pack: Economics

Scenarios for a micro or macro economics course. The student reasons about incentives, markets, and tradeoffs, and avoids the classic fallacies.

## Scenario entities and decisions

- A market where a shock shifts supply or demand and the student predicts price and quantity.
- A firm or person deciding at the margin, weighing the next unit's benefit against its cost.
- A comparison of who should produce what, using comparative advantage.
- A policy choice (tax, subsidy, price control) and its effect on surplus and efficiency.

## Metrics, frameworks, and vocabulary (with realistic ranges)

- Micro: supply and demand, price elasticity (elastic above 1, inelastic below 1), opportunity cost, marginal benefit and marginal cost, the profit-maximizing rule (marginal revenue equals marginal cost), market structures (perfect competition, monopoly, oligopoly), externalities, consumer and producer surplus, deadweight loss, sunk cost.
- Macro: GDP, inflation, unemployment, fiscal versus monetary policy, comparative versus absolute advantage.

## What good distractors look like here

- Confusing a movement along a curve (a price change) with a shift of the curve (a change in a determinant).
- The sunk-cost fallacy: letting unrecoverable past spending drive a forward-looking decision.
- Confusing absolute advantage (who produces more) with comparative advantage (who has the lower opportunity cost).
- Getting the elasticity intuition backward, or reading a macro correlation as causation.
- Ignoring opportunity cost by counting only out-of-pocket cost.

## House style: two worked examples

Scenario, medium: "The price of coffee rises and quantity demanded falls. A student says 'demand decreased.' What is the precise correction?" Correct answer: demand did not decrease; the higher price caused a movement along the demand curve to a lower quantity demanded. Demand shifts only when a determinant other than the good's own price changes. Distractors: agree that demand fell (the error), say supply fell, say the curve is vertical.

Scenario, hard: "Country X can make both cloth and wine using fewer hours than Country Y, but its advantage in wine is far larger. Who should specialize in cloth, and why?" Correct answer: Country Y should make cloth; even though X has absolute advantage in both, Y has the lower opportunity cost in cloth, so comparative advantage drives specialization and gains from trade. Distractors: X makes both (ignores opportunity cost), neither should trade, decide by absolute advantage.

## Feedback voice

Correct-answer feedback names the principle (movement versus shift, comparative advantage, marginal reasoning). Incorrect-answer feedback names the fallacy or confusion the option represents, without restating the answer.
