# Principles

The guardrails that make AI-assisted teaching defensible. Read this before you run any skill on real student data.

## 1. AI drafts, you decide

No skill in this toolkit produces a final grade, a sanction, or a student-facing message on its own. Every one ends by handing you something to review. If you find yourself approving outputs without reading them, stop. The human-in-the-loop is not a formality. It is the thing that makes this ethical and the thing that catches the AI's mistakes.

## 2. Minimize what you feed the model

You do not need to paste a student's full record to score one assignment. Send the least data required for the task. Strip names where the task does not need them. Prefer IDs or initials in prompts you paste into consumer tools. For anything sensitive, use a tool with a data-protection agreement your institution has approved, not a personal account.

## 3. Tell students how AI is used

If AI helps score participation or flag work for review, your students should know that, in plain language, in your syllabus. Transparency is not just polite. It is what lets a student challenge a result, which is their right. Hidden AI grading is the fastest way to lose trust and the fastest way to a complaint you cannot defend.

## 4. AI flags, humans sanction

An AI may surface a possible problem: a participation outlier, a suspicious citation, an answer that does not match the key. It must never be the thing that penalizes the student. You investigate the flag. You make the call. "The AI said so" is not a standard you would accept from a student, so do not hold yourself to it either.

## 5. Check the AI the way you teach students to check it

The same rigor you teach (verify the numbers, read a sample by hand, test the edges) applies to your own use. When a skill summarizes a whole class or audits an exam, spot-check it. The toolkit is built to make that check fast. Do not skip it because the output looks confident. Looking confident is exactly what these models do when they are wrong.

## 6. Bias and fairness

AI scoring can encode bias, especially against students who write in a second language, use non-standard phrasing, or come from outside the cultural defaults the model was trained on. The grading-assistant skill exists partly to catch this in your favor (crediting correct-but-differently-worded answers), but the same mechanism can cut the other way. Review flagged items with that risk in mind, and watch for patterns in who gets flagged.

## 7. Keep your records

When AI assists a grade, keep what it produced and what you changed. If a grade is ever challenged, "here is the activity log, here is the AI summary, here is my adjustment and why" is a strong position. An unrecorded gut call is not.

## 8. Generic by design

This toolkit ships with no real student names, no institution branding, and no identifying course data. If you adapt it, keep your students' data out of anything you publish or share. Use the placeholders.
