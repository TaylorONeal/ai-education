# I gave AI a teaching assistant's job, not a teacher's job

Near the end of last term I was scoring class participation. One student had posted nothing in our class chat all semester. On paper, that is a zero.

So I had AI pull that student's full activity instead of trusting my memory of the channel. It came back with seven long private message threads with me and my TA. Real questions, real follow-up, the kind of engagement I would want from anyone. The student was not checked out. They were just quiet in public.

A quick scan of the chat would have graded that student unfairly, and I would never have known. The AI caught it. Then I made the call. That gap, between what the tool surfaces and what I decide, is the whole point of how I use AI in my classroom, and it is what I want to talk about here.

I teach digital analytics to college juniors and seniors. Over the last year I have leaned on AI for a lot of the job. Not to teach for me. To take the busywork off my plate so I have more time for students, and to build things for those students that I could not have built alone. One rule sits under all of it: AI does the draft, I make the call, and nothing reaches a student without me reading it first.

## The boring work that quietly eats your week

None of this is teaching. It is overhead. But overhead is exactly what steals the hours you would rather spend on the actual job.

I post a lot of class updates, and they all need to sound like me, not like a robot. So I had AI study a few years of my old announcements and learn my voice, my sign-offs, the jokes I reuse. Now it drafts in my voice, runs a pass that scrubs out the tells that scream "a machine wrote this," and hands me something that already reads like me. I edit it and post it. Twenty minutes a post became two.

My school's tool for posting assignment pages is clunky, and making a page look halfway decent by hand takes forever. I set up a template once. Now I write the assignment in plain text and get back a clean, finished page. The time I used to burn on formatting goes into writing clearer instructions, which is the part students actually feel.

And the small one that matters more than it sounds: students ask questions at 11pm. I cannot always be there, and neither can my TA. So I set up a watcher. If a question sits too long, it nudges me and hands me a draft reply pulled from the course materials. I send it, fix it, or ignore it. It never sends on its own.

Notice the shape of all three. The machine does the assembling. I do the judging. Nothing goes out that I have not read.

## The part I am most careful about: grading

Here AI is a teaching assistant, not a replacement. It applies the rubric I wrote, then double-checks me.

Its most useful move is catching the student who clearly understood the material but said it in different words than my answer key expected. That student used to quietly lose points whenever I was grading the bottom of a tall stack at midnight. A student writing in their second language used to lose points for phrasing, not for knowledge. Now those answers get flagged for me to look at again. I still decide every grade. The AI just makes me a more consistent and fairer grader than a tired human can be alone.

I will be honest about participation, because it is the one I am least proud of historically. For years my participation grades were basically a feeling about who seemed engaged. That is not fair, and deep down I knew it. Now I have a record: every student's real activity, public and private, scored on rules I set in advance, in a summary I read line by line before anything counts. Same effort from me. But now the grade is backed by something I can show a student who asks.

There is also the exam problem every teacher knows. You cannot tell if a test is too easy or too brutal until students have already taken it, and by then it is too late. So now, before anyone sees it, I have AI play the test. It role-plays different kinds of student, the over-preparer, the crammer, the one who misreads, and walks through the exam. That gives me a rough sense of the score spread before it counts for real. When the spread comes back lopsided, I rebalance. The students walk into a test that has already been pressure-tested instead of being the ones who discover it was unfair.

## Teaching students to use AI the right way

I do not ban AI in my class. I do something harder. I build assignments where using it lazily blows up in your face.

The first thing I teach them is that it lies. AI sounds confident even when it is flat wrong. It will invent a statistic and cite a study that does not exist. I show them real examples: AI insisting a number grew 42 percent when their own data clearly says 28, or quoting a report that was never written. I want them suspicious before they are impressed.

Then I make them work in steps on their own real data. Make the AI describe the data back to you before it analyzes anything, because most of its mistakes come from bluffing about numbers it never really looked at. Make it show its math, then redo the key number a second way to see if the two answers match. Run the same question through a second AI tool, because two tools that agree is your best safety signal and two that disagree means you go back to the source.

I even grade them on arguing with it. Students are weirdly polite to AI and accept whatever it gives them. So one assignment rewards pushing back: if it labels everything the same, you tell it to recheck and do better. They are the analyst. The AI is the intern.

And they own the result. "The AI wrote it" is not an excuse, the same way "the calculator did it" was never an excuse. You turned it in. It is yours.

## The test I run on everything

Two questions, on every single use. Does this give my students more of me, or less of me? And does it give them something they could not get any other way? If a tool makes me more distant, I do not use it. If it gives students an experience they would otherwise wait years to get, I find a way.

I am not really teaching them this year's chatbots, which will be old news by the time they graduate. I am teaching them to treat any AI like a sharp but unreliable assistant whose work you always check. The tools will change. That habit will not.
